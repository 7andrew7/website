Wireless Research at UW

FAQ:
  • What's with all the antennas?

    These computers are equipped with the state of the art wireless network interface chips that implement a draft of the new IEEE 802.11n standard for next-generation high-throughput wireless networking. The focus of this standard is multi-antenna communication. The use of multiple antennas adds reliability, improves range, and can increase the speed of a link proportional to the number of antennas in the best case.

  • Do these cards emit dangerous levels of radiation?

    No. Although the computers have more (visible) antennas than you may be used to, the amount of power transmitted is no more than that emitted by your laptop or iPhone wireless, and is much less than that of your CDMA or GSM cell phone connection, or microwave. Also, the testbed is typically idle during the day. Don't worry.

  • Does the testbed interfere with normal wireless usage in the Allen Center?

    No. We operate our testbed on an unused channel in the 5.2 GHz frequency band. At the time of deployment, no CSE, UW, or EE access points share this frequency.

  • What wireless activity does the testbed measure and/or record?

    We are interested in understanding how well the new 802.11n wireless works and developing novel algorithms, methods, techniques, etcetera. We mainly measure the physical scattering properties of wireless links within CSE over time and space. We do not record any identifying information about any transmissions that are not both from and to our testbed nodes. In addition, as no service-providing access points operate on the wireless channel we use, no personal traffic other than scanning for access points should ever even be overheard (and subsequently not recorded) by our testbed.
